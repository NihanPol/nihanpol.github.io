# A short introduction to Neutron stars

## What is a neutron star?

A neutron star is literally a star made up of (mostly) free neutrons. These stars, on average, weigh 1.4 times as much as 
our Sun, but have an approximate radius of only 15 km. To put this in perspective, imagine the entire mass of the Sun
packed into a sphere that would fit snugly inside Manhattan, New York City. This makes these stars some of the most 
dense objects found in the Universe, with a teaspoon of neutron star stuff weighing about 900 times the mass of the Great
Pyramid of Giza.

## How is a neutron star formed?

When a star, weighing more than about 8 times our Sun, runs out of fuel, it cannot support itself against its own 
gravitational pull. As a result, the star collapses in on itself which results in the formation of a
neutron star accompanied by a supernova explosion. If the mass of this remnant exceeds about 3 times the mass of our Sun,
then it again collapses in on itself to form a black hole.

At the end of this formation process, the radius of the neutron star is a tiny fraction of that of the star from which it
formed. A combination of this fact and conservation of angular momentum work together to spin this newly formed neutron 
star up to very high rotational frequencies, or conversely, very small rotational periods.

## How do we see these neutron stars?

In 1967, then graduate student, Dame Jocelyn Bell discovered periodic radio pulses coming from an unkown source in the sky.
She, along with collaborators, determined that this periodic pulsed emission was likely coming from neutron stars. Since
they emit this pulsed radio emission, these neutron stars are called "pulsars". This hypothesis was confirmed 
with subsequent discoveries of this type of emission, especially that from the Crab pulsar.

Most of the pulsars that have been observed have been discovered using radio telescopes such as the Parkes radio telescope in
Australia or the Arecibo radio telescope in Puerto Rico, USA. I make use of the Green Bank Telescope in Green Bank, WV, USA 
to observe pulsars. Pulsars have also been observed in other wavelength bands such as optical, X-rays and gamma rays.
