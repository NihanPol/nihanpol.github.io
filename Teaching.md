# Teaching

I love teaching! One of my long-term professional goals is to obtain a position that allows me to teach along with carrying out my research.

I was a teaching assistant in the Physics and Astronomy department at West Virginia University (WVU) for a year. During this year, I taught 6 lab sections, each consisting of about 40 undergraduate students each. This lab was part of the Physics 101 course at WVU, so we discussed basic concepts in Physics and how we can conduct experiments to test these concepts. In addition to being a teaching assistant, I have also been a guest lecturer for three different sections of Physics 101, one section of Astronomy 106 (introductory astronomy) and for one section of Astronomy 298A (special topics in astronomy).

Apart from teaching Physics and Astronomy, I also like to teach software programming and computational techniques. Learning how to use different programming languages, especially Python, is one the big reasons that I am where I am doing cool science. I would love to help pass on this programming knowledge to other people so that they can use it to their benefit. I teach programming through [The Carpentries](https://carpentries.org/) organization, whose goal is to teach foundational coding, and data science skills to researchers worldwide. I am a trained instructor with the Carpentries, which means I can volunteer to teach any of their software, data or library carpentry workshops that are being organized around the US. So far, I have taught six software carpentry workshops, all of which have been extremely fun to teach and have introduced me to researchers from a wide range of disciplines and institutes. These workshops have taught me how different fields of research have their own unique data and the innovative ways in which they approach the analysis of their data using computational techniques to produce amazing new results. 

I have taught software carpentry workshops at the following institutes:

  - (Workshop 1](https://nihanpol.github.io/2018-01-27-wvu/)
  - [Workshop 2](https://ncf0003.github.io/2019-10-26-wvu/)

Apart from teaching with The Carpentries, I have also been working with the Research Commons and Libraries at WVU to expand the computational expertise at WVU. As part of this, I have taught two software carpentry workshops at WVU as well as volunteered to assist with other workshops such as, for example, those that taught the statistical programming language, R, with which I am not familiar. I also designed and taught a one-day [intermediate-level Python workshop](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/intermediate-python-workshop-tickets-46106041411) at WVU as a follow-up to the more introductory software carpentry workshops mentioned above. I try to actively maintain this intermediate-level workshop curriculum, which I am planning to teach again in Spring 2020 at WVU. I am also in the process of developing an introduction to machine learning with Python course following a similar structure and methodology as the software carpentry workshops.

# Outreach

I enjoy doing outreach and communicating science to the general audience. Since all of my research is funded through taxpayer money, I feel it is absolutely necessary to communicate the results of our research to the taxpayer. All of my outreach so far has been through events organized by WVU or North American Nanohertz Obervatory for Gravitational waves (NANOGrav).

One such outreach event with WVU was the [Community Physics festival](https://eberly.wvu.edu/news-events/events/072019community-physics-festival), organized by the Physics and Astronomy graduate student organization, at which I gave a public talk about building a Galaxy-scale gravitational wave detector with NANOGrav. I also volunteered at [Yuri's night](https://eberly.wvu.edu/news-events/events/yuri-s-night) event at WVU, which was a worldwide celebration of humanity's past, present and future in space, where I operated the telescope viewing stations consisting of small Dobsonian optical telescopes.

I have also participated in outreach through NANOGrav, which has mostly consisted of hosting outreach booths at different conferences and exhibitions. One such event was the [National Science and Engineering festival](https://usasciencefestival.org/) in Washington DC in 2016. At this event, we had a booth setup to explain the science of NANOGrav to the hundreds of people, including middle- and high-school students.
